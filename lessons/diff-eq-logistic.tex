\begin{document} \section{The logistic model}

We develop and analyze a more realistic population model called the \hlmain{logistic equation}.  It is
\begin{itemize}
  \item sophisticated enough to account for \emph{crowding}, 
  \item simple enough that it can be modified to account for additional factors, and
  \item one of two fundamental models in biology. The other one is called the Lotka-Volterra model.
\end{itemize}

In Example~\ref{ex:diff-eq-intro}, we discussed the population model \(N'(t) = (B - D) N(t)\) with the assumption that \emph{per capita birth and death rates} were \underline{\hspace{2in}}.  This assumption makes the math simple but not terribly realistic.  We demonstrate how a crowding constraint can be incorporated into a model by retracing the development of the logistic model.  The following is taken from \emph{How Ecologists Develop the Logistic Model} by Pfaff, PRIMUS (\url{https://doi.org/10.1080/10511970.2024.2310249}).

\blanklines{30}
\clearpage

\begin{definition}[logistic model]
  The population model \(N(t)\) with a growth factor \(r > 0\) and a carrying capacity \(K\) satisfies the differential equation
  \begin{equation} \label{eq:diff-eq-logistic}
    N'(t) = r N(t) \left( 1 - \frac{K}{N(t)} \right).
  \end{equation}

  The carrying capacity \(K\) is a constant representing the size of a population that the modelled environment can sustain over time.
\end{definition}

We advertised that the logistic model accounts for the crowding effect. Is it really true?
