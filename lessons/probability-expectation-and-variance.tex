\begin{document} \section{Expectation and variance}

Probability describes a big picture view of data. Pictorially, probability mass/density functions help us visualize data. Other common ways of thinking about data involves computing their \hlmain{expectation} and \hlmain{variance}. 

\begin{definition}[expectation]
  The \hlmain{expectation} of a discrete random variable \(X\) with PMF \(f_{X}(x)\) and values \(x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n}\) is
  \begin{equation} \label{eq:expectation-discrete}
    E(X) = \sum_{i = 1}^{n} x_{i} f_{X}(x_{i}).
  \end{equation}

  The \hlmain{expectation} of a continuous random variable \(X\) with PDF \(f(x)\) is
  \begin{equation} \label{eq:expectation-continuous}
    E(X) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x f(x) \;dx.
  \end{equation}
\end{definition}

Variance is defined as \(Var(X) = E( (X - E(x))^{2} )\) regardless of whether a random variable \(X\) is discrete or continuous. However, this formula needs further explanation because it does not \emph{directly} tell us how to calculate variance.
\begin{definition}[variance and standard deviation]
  Let \(\mu = E(x)\) which is a constant.

  The \hlmain{variance} of a discrete random variable \(X\) with PMF \(f_{X}(x)\) and values \(x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n}\) is 
  \begin{equation} \label{def:variance-discrete}
    Var(X) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_{i} - \mu)^{2} f_{X}(x_{i}).
  \end{equation}

  The \hlmain{variance} of a continuous random variable \(X\) with PDF \(f(x)\) is 
  \begin{equation} \label{def:variance-continuous}
    Var(X) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} (x - \mu)^{2} f(x) \;dx.
  \end{equation}

  The \hlmain{standard deviation} (for both types) is \(\sigma(X) = \sqrt{Var(X)}\).

  It is quite common to denote standard deviation by \(\sigma\) or \(s\) and variance by \(\sigma^{2}\) or \(s^{2}\).
\end{definition}

Examples~\ref{ex:drv-shapes}~and~\ref{ex:crv-shapes} demonstrate the general observation that \hlmain{expectation \(\mu\), variance \(\sigma^{2}\) and standard deviation \(\sigma\)} together roughly describe the \hlmain{shape of a random variable}.  

Sometimes (in reality and in this course), we want to randomly generate data that replicate experiments with certain sample mean and sample variance.

\begin{definition}[uniform distribution]
  The uniform distribution \(U(a,b)\) is defined by its probability density function
  \[
    f(x) = 
    \begin{cases}
      \frac{1}{b-a}, &\text{ if } a \le x \le b \\[2ex]
      0,            &\text{ otherwise }.
    \end{cases}
  \]

  Its expectation is \(\mu = \frac{1}{2}(b - a)\) and variance is \(\frac{1}{12}(b - a)^{2}\).
\end{definition}

Depending on the capabilities of your software and the requirements of your simulation/experiment, you may need to do a little conversion between specifying a uniform distribution by an interval \(U(a,b)\) or by its expectation and standard deviation.

\begin{method}
  The uniform distribution \(U(-c,c)\) has expectation and standard deviation
  \[
    \mu = 0
    \quad\text{and}\quad
    \sigma = \frac{c}{\sqrt{3}}.
  \]

  Conversely, if you need a uniform distribution with expectation \(\mu = 0\) and standard deviation \(\sigma\), then 
  \[
    c = \sigma \sqrt{3}
    \quad\text{with PDF}\quad
    f(x) = 
    \begin{cases}
      \frac{1}{2\sigma \sqrt{3}} &\text{if } -c \le x \le c, \\[2ex]
      0 &\text{otherwise}.
    \end{cases}
  \]
\end{method}
