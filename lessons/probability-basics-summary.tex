\begin{document} \section{Summary of the fundamentals of probability}

Calculating probability from the \hlmain{first principle} is a counting problem. Determine the number of outcomes in an event \(E\) and the total number of outcomes in the sample space and use the definition
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb{P}(E) = \frac{\text{number of outcomes in \(E\)}}{\text{total number of outcomes in \(\Omega\)}}. \tag{\ref{eq:probability}}
\end{equation}

Given two events \(E,F\) in a common sample space \(\Omega\), we have the \hlmain{principle of inclusion-exclusion}
\begin{align}
  \mathbb{P}(E \cup F) 
  &= \mathbb{P}(E) + \mathbb{P}(F) - \mathbb{P}(E \cap F). \tag{\ref{eq:probability-cup-cap}}
\end{align}

Given an event \(E\), we can compute the probability of its \hlmain{complement}
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb{P}(E^{c}) = 1 - \mathbb{P}(E) = \frac{\text{number of outcomes not in \(E\)}}{\text{total number of outcomes in \(\Omega\)}}. 
  \tag{\ref{eq:probability-comp}}
\end{equation}

Two events \(E,F\) are called mutually exclusive if \(E \cap F = \emptyset\). In other words, two mutually exclusive events have nothing in common and
\[
\mathbb{P}(E \cup F) = \mathbb{P}(E) + \mathbb{P}(F) \quad\text{ if and only if}\quad \text{\(E\) and \(F\) are mutually exclusive}.
\]

Events \(E_{1}, E_{2}, \ldots, E_{n}\) are \hlmain{mutually exclusive and exhaustive} if \(E_{i} \cap E_{j} = \emptyset\) for all \(i \ne j\), and are \hlmain{exhaustive} if \(E_{1} \cup \cdots \cup E_{n} = \Omega\).  In term of probability, events \(E_{1}, \ldots, E_{n}\) are \hlmain{mutually exclusive and exhaustive} if \(\mathbb{P}(E_{i} \cap E_{j} = 0\) for all \(i \ne j\) and \(\mathbb{P}(E_{1} \cup \cdots E_{n}) = 1\).

A \hlmain{conditional probability} is the probability of an event \(E\) \hlmain{given} that an event \(F\) has already happened 
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb{P}(E \mid F) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(E \cap F)}{\mathbb{P}(F)} = \frac{\text{number of outcomes in \(E\) and \(F\)}}{\text{number of outcomes in \(F\) only}}.
\end{equation}

An application of conditional probability is errors in medical testing. We have four probabilities and an associated table.
\[
  \begin{array}{cclcl}
    \text{specificity} 
  &=& \mathbb{P}(\text{true negatives}) 
  &=& \mathbb{P}( \text{negatives} \mid \text{not present} ) \\
  \mathbb{P}(\text{type-I errors})
  &=& \mathbb{P}(\text{false positives}) 
  &=& \mathbb{P}( \text{positives} \mid \text{not present} ) \\
    \text{sensitivity} 
  &=& \mathbb{P}(\text{true positives}) 
  &=& \mathbb{P}( \text{positives} \mid \text{present} ) \\
  \mathbb{P}(\text{type-II errors})
  &=& \mathbb{P}(\text{false negative}) 
  &=& \mathbb{P}( \text{negatives} \mid \text{present} ) \\
  \end{array}
\]

\begin{center}
  \begin{tabular}{c||c|c}
      & \parbox{1.25in}{\centering condition\\present} & \parbox{1.25in}{\centering condition\\not present} \\\midrule\midrule
    \(+\) &   true positive    & false positive \\[2ex]\midrule
    \(-\) &   false negative   & true negative \\[2ex]
  \end{tabular}
\end{center}

The next two are part of the fundamentals of probability theory but is not on the midterm test.

The \hlmain{law of total probability} states that if \(F_{1}, \ldots, F_{n}\) are mutually exclusive and exhaustive events of a sample space \(\Omega\), then 
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb{P}(E) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \mathbb{P}(E \mid F_{i}) \mathbb{P}(F_{i}).
\end{equation}

Two events are independent if 
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb{P}(E \mid F) = \mathbb{P}(E)
  \quad\text{or, equivalently,}\quad
  \mathbb{P}(E \cap F) = \mathbb{P}(E) \mathbb{P}(F).
  \tag{\ref{eq:probability-independence}}
\end{equation}
