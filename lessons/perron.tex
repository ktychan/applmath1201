A hidden pattern for the long-term behaviour emerges when we \emph{arbitrarily} change the initial condition.
\[
  \begin{array}{rclcrclc}
    A^{100} \begin{bmatrix} 0.5 \\ 0.5 \end{bmatrix} &\approx& \begin{bmatrix} 0.615 \\ 0.385 \end{bmatrix}, &\dots&
    A^{10000} \begin{bmatrix} 0.5 \\ 0.5 \end{bmatrix} &\approx& \begin{bmatrix} 0.615 \\ 0.385 \end{bmatrix}, &\dots \\[2ex]
    A^{100} \begin{bmatrix} 0.1 \\ 0.1 \end{bmatrix} &\approx& \begin{bmatrix} 0.123 \\ 0.077 \end{bmatrix}, &\dots&
    A^{10000} \begin{bmatrix} 0.1 \\ 0.1 \end{bmatrix} &\approx& \begin{bmatrix} 0.123 \\ 0.077 \end{bmatrix}, &\dots \\[2ex]
    A^{100} \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -0.1 \end{bmatrix} &\approx& \begin{bmatrix} 1.785 \\ 1.115 \end{bmatrix}, &\dots&
    A^{10000} \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -0.1 \end{bmatrix} &\approx& \begin{bmatrix} 1.785 \\ 1.115 \end{bmatrix}, &\dots
  \end{array}
\]

The pattern: The ratios \(0.385/0.615\), \(0.077 / 0.123\), \(1.115 / 1.785\) \hlwarn{Edited: ``\(1.115 / 0.175\)'' \(\to\) ``\(1.115 / 1.785\)'' (the vector was \([1.785, 1.115]\))} \hlwarn{FIXME: verify this math/content correction} are all roughly \(0.625\).
Recall that \(A\) has eigenvectors 
\[
  \vec{u} = \begin{bmatrix} {\color{main} 1} \\ {\color{main} 0.625} \end{bmatrix} \text{ with eigenvalue } {\color{main} \mu = 1}
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  \vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} \text{ with eigenvalue } \lambda = 0.35.
\]

% Regardless of the initial condition, the final solution for \(\vec{p}(t+1) = A\vec{p}(t)\) has the form
% \[
%   \vec{p}(t) = a (1)^{n} {\color{main} \vec{u}} + b (0.35)^{n} \vec{v} = a (1)^{t}  \begin{bmatrix} {\color{main} 1} \\ {\color{main} 0.625} \end{bmatrix}+ b (0.35)^{t} \vec{v}
%   \quad\text{where}\quad
%   \vec{p}(0) = a \vec{u} + b \vec{v}.

The above pattern is not a coincidence and helps us \hlwarn{Edited: ``help us'' \(\to\) ``helps us''} \emph{quickly} determine long-term behaviour for (vector) recurrence.

\begin{method}[long-term behaviour for recurrence equations]
  The \hlmain{dominant eigenvalue} \(\mu\) is the largest eigenvalue, and a \hlmain{dominant eigenvector} is an eigenvector whose associated eigenvalue is \(\mu\).

  For models \(\vec{x}(t+1) = A \vec{x}(t)\) with a ``nice enough'' matrix\footnote{We are not required to know what ``nice enough'' means. Intuitively, it means the subpopulations don't have isolated subgroups.} \(A\), the long-term behaviour \(\lim_{t \to \infty} \vec{x}(t)\) \hlmain{lies in the direction of the dominant eigenvector}. When \(A\) models populations, then its dominant eigenvalue \(\mu\) is always positive, and 
  \begin{itemize}
    \item if \(\mu > 1\), all subpopulations grow unbounded with fixed ratio.
    \item if \(\mu = 1\), all subpopulations stabilize.
    \item if \(\mu < 1\), all subpopulations go extinct.
  \end{itemize}
\end{method}

What happens if we model \hlwarn{Edited: ``if model'' \(\to\) ``if we model''} something other than (absolute) populations such as particle movement?  We have similar \hlwarn{Edited: removed duplicated ``similar''} conclusions when eigenvalues are complex.

\faStar{} Suppose we have a vector recursion \(\vec{x}(t+1) = A\vec{x}(t)\) \hlwarn{Edited: ``\(A\vec{x}\)'' \(\to\) ``\(A\vec{x}(t)\)''} where the \(2 \times 2\) matrix \(A\) has complex eigenvalues \(a \pm bi\).  Then the matrix \(A\) is ``essentially'' 
\(A = \begin{bmatrix} a & -b\\ b & a \end{bmatrix} \).
In this case, the vector field for the (vector) recurrence is always a spiral pattern.
\begin{multicols}{2}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item If \(a > 1\), the arrows spiral outward. 
    \item If \(a = 1\), the arrows trace perfect concentric circles.
    \item If \(a < 1\), the arrows spiral inward.
      \columnbreak
    \item If \(b > 0\), the arrows spiral counterclockwise.
    \item If \(b < 0\), the arrows spiral clockwise.
  \end{itemize}
\end{multicols}
