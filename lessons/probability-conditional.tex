\begin{document} \section{Conditional probability}

We now consider probability of an event \hlmain{given} that another event has already happened.   We consider a concrete example from the first principle to motivate the formula associated to conditional probability.

\begin{example} \label{ex:probability-conditional-motivation}
  Let \(\Omega = \{1,2,3,4,5,6\}\) be the sample space of six people.  Suppose a magical new test can detect whether a person is a morning person.
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \(1,2,3,4\) are morning people,
    \item \(5,6\) are not morning people,
    \item \(1,3,5\) tested positive,
    \item \(2,4,6\) tested negative.
  \end{itemize}

  What is the probability that the test returns positive for someone who is not a morning person?

  \blanklines{20}
\end{example}

\begin{definition}[conditional probability]
  Suppose \(E,F\) are events inside a common sample space \(\Omega\).  The probability of \hlmain{\(E\) given \(F\)} is the probability of \(E \cap F\) inside the smaller sample space \(F\) and
  \begin{equation} \label{eq:conditional}
    \mathbb{P}( E \mid F ) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(E \cap F)}{\mathbb{P}(F)}.
  \end{equation}

  The probability \(\mathbb{P}( E \mid F )\) is called a \hlmain{conditional probability} because it represents the probability of the event \(E\) under the condition that \(F\) has already happened.
\end{definition}
\faExclamationTriangle{} Distinguish between \(\mathbb{P}(A \text{ and } B)\) and \(\mathbb{P}(A \text{ given } B)\) when describing your observations and when interpreting a question.
\clearpage

\begin{example}
  Suppose, within a population, that the probability of having green eyes is \(10\%\), the probability of having brown hair is \(75\%\), and the probability of having both green eyes and brown hair is \(9\%\). What is the probability of having brown hair given that a person has green eyes?

  \blanklines{10}
\end{example}

\begin{example}
  A test for an infectious disease was conducted on patients whose infection status is known.  \(60\%\) of the patients are infected. \(30\%\) of the patients tested negative. \(90\%\) of the infected patients also tested positive.  What is the probability that that the test returns negative for patients without an infection?

  \blanklines{20}
\end{example}

  Suppose \(\mathbb{P}(E \cup F) = 0.9\), \(\mathbb{P}(E) = 0.7\), \(\mathbb{P}(E \cap F) = 0.5\). Calculate \(\mathbb{P}(E \mid F)\).

  \blanklines{15}
\end{exercise}
