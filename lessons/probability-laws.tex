However, enumerating outcomes in a sample space may or may not be desirable. Probability laws give us algebraic relations among events related by set relations.

It is often natural to group outcomes by non-overlapping characteristic, e.g., grouping of patients by age group. The probability term is \hlmain{mutually exclusive} and \hlmain{exhaustive} events.

\begin{definition}[mutually exclusive and exhaustive]
  Two events \(E, F\) in a sample space \(\Omega\) are \hlmain{mutually exclusive} if \(E \cap F\) is empty. 

  Events \(E_{1}, \ldots, E_{n}\) in a sample space \(\Omega\) are \hlmain{mutually exclusive and exhaustive} if \hlattn{every} outcome in \(\Omega\) belongs to \hlattn{exactly one} of these events, no outcome is left behind and no outcome outside of the sample space is included. The precise relations are
  \[
    \Omega = E_{1} \cup \cdots \cup E_{n} \quad\text{ and }\quad E_{i} \cap E_{j} = \emptyset \text{ for all } i \ne j. 
  \]
\end{definition}

\begin{example}
  Let \(\Omega = \{1,2,3,4,5,6\}\) be the sample space. Which events are mutually exclusive?

  \begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
    \item \(E_{1} = \{2,5\}\)
    \item \(E_{2} = \{3,2\}\)
    \item \(E_{3} = \{6,3\}\)
    \item \(E_{4} = \{2,3,5,6\}\)
    \item \(E_{5} = \{1\}\)

We can recognize mutually exclusive and exhaustive events from relations of their probabilities.
\begin{example}
  Assume \(E_{1}, E_{2}\) are events in a sample space \(\Omega\).  Which of the following \hlwarn{Edited: ``followings'' \(\to\) ``following''; added missing ``?''} \emph{together} imply that \(E_{1}, E_{2}\) are mutually exclusive and exhaustive?
  \begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
    \item \(\mathbb{P}(E_{1} \cap E_{2}) = 0\).
    \item \(\mathbb{P}(E_{1} \cap E_{2}) = \mathbb{P}(E_{1}) + \mathbb{P}(E_{2})\).
    \item \(\mathbb{P}(E_{1} \cup E_{2}) = \mathbb{P}(E_{1}) + \mathbb{P}(E_{2})\).
    \item \(\mathbb{P}(E_{1} \cup E_{2}) = 0\).
    \item \(\mathbb{P}(E_{1} \cup E_{2}) = 1\).
  \end{enumerate}
\end{example}
