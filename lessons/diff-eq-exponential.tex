\begin{document} \section{Differential equations, exponential}

One of the simplest differential equation is \(u' = ku\) where \(k\) is a constant. 

\begin{method}
  The solution to the differential equation \(\frac{du}{dt} = k u\) where \(k\) is the proportionality constant is \(u(t) = u(0) e^{kt}\) where \(u(0)\) is the initial condition.
\end{method}

The humble exponential function can be used to model quite a few useful scenarios. However, be very careful when identifying the proportionality constant \(k\). Unless the problem directly tells you something is the proportionality constant, don't assume some number is the proportionality constant.

\begin{example} \label{eg:exponential-metabolism}
  Suppose that \(100\) micrograms of a drug is administered into the bloodstream at \(t = 0\) hour.  Assume the rate of change of the amount of the drug in the blood stream is proportional to the amount of the drug in the blood stream.  If the amount of the drug decreases by \(15\%\) after an hour, how much drug is in the blood stream after \(3 hours\)?

  \blanklines{35}
\end{example}
\clearpage

\begin{exercise}
  Suppose that \(100\) micrograms of a drug is administered into the bloodstream at \(t = 0\) hour.  Assume the rate of change of the amount of the drug in the blood stream is proportional to the amount of the drug in the blood stream.  An hour later, \(90\) micrograms of the drug is detected in the blood stream. How much drug is in the blood stream after \(45\) minutes? After \(3\) hours?

  \blanklines{50}
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[challenge of the week]
  Compare the discrete-time model \(u_{t} = a u_{t-1}\) and the continuous-time model \(y' = ky\). Both of their solutions are exponential functions \(u_{t} = u_{0} a^{t}\) and \(y(t) = y(0) e^{kt}\).

  Are the two models interchangeable?  What happens if you try to develop a discrete-time model for Example~\ref{eg:exponential-metabolism}?  Should the two models match?
\end{exercise}
