\begin{document} \section{Age-structured population}

We can use vectors and matrices to model multi-dimensional phenomena, such as age-structured population. \hlwarn{Edited: repaired a broken sentence --- ``vector and matricies [missing verb] multi-dimensional phenomenon'' \(\to\) ``vectors and matrices to model multi-dimensional phenomena''}  By age-structured population, we mean a population with two mutually exclusive subgroups \emph{adults} and \emph{juveniles} and consider the effect of growing up.

\begin{example} \label{eg:age-structure-model}
  Consider a population of juveniles and adults. Initially, there are \(20\) juveniles and \(40\) adults. At the end of each year, 
  \begin{itemize}[itemsep={0ex}]
    \item \(20\%\) of the juveniles become adults, and the rest of the juveniles die, \hlwarn{Edited: ``the rest juveniles'' \(\to\) ``the rest of the juveniles''}
    \item \(90\%\) of the adults survive, and the rest of adults die, and
    \item only adults produce offspring, \hlwarn{Edited: ``offsprings'' \(\to\) ``offspring''} and the per-capita birth rate is \(15\%\).
  \end{itemize}

  Predict the number of adults and juveniles after \(2\) years.  How would you predict the populations at the end of \(n\) years?
